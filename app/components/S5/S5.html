How Does Your Diet Shape Up?

  • Review your diet sheet and the Food Pyramids for Day 1 and 2. To find out how healthy your diet using the following questions:

    • Did you get a balance of foods from each of the Food Pyramid groups?

    Did you choose whole grains?

  • Complex carbohydrates give long-lasting energy, try oatmeal, brown rice, whole wheat bread, and popcorn for more fibre and nutritional value. Try quinoa, millet, bulgur, and amaranth for an interesting change.

    • How many fruits and vegetables did you eat?

  • 150ml of fruit juice counts as a maximum of one portion of fruit, so however much fruit juice you drink in a day, it only counts as one portion of fruit. You need at least five servings of fruits and vegetables each day. If you consume less than five, try adding some at each meal. For example, at breakfast, drink a berries smoothie or add an apple to your cereal; for lunch, pack along some carrot sticks and a piece of fruit, order a salad, or add lettuce and tomato slices to your sandwiches; eat fruit when you want a sweet and thirst-quenching snack, and don’t skip the veggies at dinnertime. Keep fruits and veggies handy at work or home.

    • Did you eat breakfast?

  • Breakfasts with protein as well as carbohydrates will give you the most energy for the longest time. Choose cereals containing ‘slow-release’ carbohydrates such as porridge oats or all-bran, in preference to those with ‘fast-release’ carbohydrates such as cornflakes. Good breakfasts are porridge with non-dairy milk (such as oat milk or soya milk) with added wheatgerm; oats with live yogurt, fresh fruit, and nuts or seeds (preferably ground); eggs and wholemeal or rye bread toast; muesli or other wholegrain cereal; kippers; etc.

    • Did you feed your bones?

  • How many calcium-rich foods did you consume? It’s recommended that you eat or drink three servings of a calcium rich food per day. To get your calcium, enjoy low-fat milk, yogurt, frozen yogurt, and cheeses (part-skim mozzarella, low-fat cottage cheese, farmer cheese), calciumfortified orange juice, tofu, sardines or salmon with bones, deep green leafy vegetables such as broccoli, collards, kale, and a variety of beans, nuts and seeds. For any sushi lovers, seaweed is also a good source of calcium! NOTE: Lactose Intolerance. Some people have trouble drinking milk because they are “lactose intolerant.” This means that they do not produce the enzyme that digests the milk sugar, lactose. Lactose intolerant people suffer from bloating, stomach-aches, and nausea soon after consuming products containing milk. Be sure to check labels since milk, lactose, whey, and other milk components are often added in processed foods and may give you trouble as well. If you have lactose intolerance, try eating non-dairy calcium sources such as tofu and spinach

    • Did you eat sufficient amounts of essential fatty acids?

  • Essential fatty acids are found in flaxseeds, hemp seeds, pumpkin, sunflower and sesame seeds and oils, canola, wheat germ and soybean oils, nuts (such as walnuts and brazil nuts), dark green leafy vegetables and oily fish such as mackerel, sardines, anchovies and albacore tuna. It is now frequently recommended to consume at least two portions of oily fish per week.

    • Did you eat too much fat?

  • Did you add extra butter, margarine, or mayonnaise to your food? Did you eat a chips, fast foods, or fried foods? To cut down on fat, choose less fatty snacks like plain popcorn, trail mix, or fruits. Choose low-fat milk, yogurt, and frozen desserts. Go easy on fried foods, fatty meats, and creamy.
